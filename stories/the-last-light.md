The lighthouse keeper watched the sun dip below the horizon for what she believed would be the last time. Not the last sunset — those would continue long after humanity had packed its bags — but the last one she would witness from this particular cliff, this particular chair, with the scent of salt and rust mingling in the evening air.

She pressed the ignition switch out of habit. The great lamp above sputtered, caught, and began its slow rotation. A cone of amber light swept across the dark water, painting brief gold stripes on the crests of waves that would never carry another ship home.

"Still working," she murmured, as though the light owed her an explanation.

## II

The radio had gone silent three weeks ago. Before that, the messages had thinned from hourly bulletins to daily check-ins to a single voice — a woman in Reykjavik whose name she never learned — reading poetry into the static every Tuesday and Thursday. Then nothing.

She did not mourn the silence. It arrived the way winter arrives: expected, gradual, then suddenly absolute.

The supplies in the cellar would last another two months if she was disciplined. She was disciplined. The keeper had spent seventeen years measuring oil, trimming wicks, logging wind speed and wave height with the mechanical devotion of a clock. Discipline was not a virtue she practiced; it was the architecture of her days.

## III

On the fourth week of silence, a boat appeared.

It was small — a wooden dinghy with a patchwork sail — and it moved across the water with the uncertain gait of something wounded. She watched it through the telescope, her breath fogging the lens.

A boy sat at the tiller. Twelve, maybe thirteen. He wore a coat three sizes too large, and his eyes, when the lamp's beam crossed his face, were open and unafraid.

She descended the spiral stairs, two hundred and twelve steps counting the cracked one, and walked to the dock. The boy tied off with a sailor's knot that spoke of teaching, not instinct.

"Is the light still on?" he asked.

"You can see it is."

"Good." He climbed onto the dock with the careful gravity of someone carrying something fragile. "People are going to need it."

She looked at the empty sea behind him. "What people?"

He met her gaze. "The ones who haven't given up yet."
