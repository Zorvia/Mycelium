The city had no name that human mouths could shape. Its spires rose from the ocean floor like the fingers of a buried giant reaching for a sky it would never touch. Bioluminescent algae clung to every surface, painting the ruins in shifting blues and greens that pulsed with the deep current like a sleeping heartbeat.

Maren adjusted her regulator and kicked deeper.

She had found the coordinates in her grandmother's journal — a water-stained notebook filled with sketches of impossible architecture and a language that looked like music notation. The old woman had been a marine biologist who, in her final years, spoke less about science and more about doors.

"There are doors at the bottom of the sea," she would say, her eyes bright and distant. "Not metaphors. Doors."

## The Descent

The pressure suit was rated to four hundred meters. The city began at three hundred and eighty. Maren felt the suit creak around her like a fist slowly closing, but the readings held. She descended along the main avenue — if you could call it that — a broad corridor flanked by columns carved with scenes of creatures that were not quite fish and not quite human.

Her lamp caught something ahead: a doorway. Not a gap in a wall or a collapsed archway, but an actual door — rectangular, framed, with hinges corroded to lace but still holding.

It was open.

Beyond it, the water was warmer. She tasted salt and something else, something sweet, like the memory of rain on hot stone.

## What She Found

The room behind the door was dry.

That should have been impossible. Three hundred and eighty meters below the surface, behind a door without a seal, a room existed in which there was no water. Maren pulled herself through the threshold and felt gravity catch her. She stumbled, caught her balance, and stood.

Air. Real air. It smelled of old paper and candle wax.

Shelves lined every wall, floor to ceiling, filled with books. Not the bloated, disintegrating remains of books — intact volumes bound in materials she could not identify, their spines lettered in the same musical notation from her grandmother's journal.

A desk sat in the center of the room. On it, a single book lay open. The page it was turned to held an illustration: a woman in a pressure suit, standing in a library at the bottom of the sea.

Maren looked at the drawing. The drawing looked back.

She sat down and began to read.
